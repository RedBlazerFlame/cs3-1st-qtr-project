This video by Tom Scott discusses how high carbon dioxide concentration negatively affects your cognition

Introduction

Breathe in. Breathe out. Breathing is an important action for our bodies. When we breathe in, we take in oxygen. Our cells need oxygen to continue functioning.



But what if the air we breathe contains impurities? Sadly, this is already the case. Carbon dioxide, a simple asphyxiant (i.e., a gas that displaces oxygen), is now more concentrated in the air we breathe. Due to industrialization, carbon dioxide concentrations are now 49% higher than normal.



So, what does this mean for our bodies, particularly our brains? The short answer is that higher carbon dioxide concentrations negatively affect our cognition. The long answer is the rest of this article.

Parts Per Million (PPM) Definition

Parts per million is a unit that measures concentration. It is defined as the "number of parts" of solute in one million parts of a solvent. Common units include: µL/L (volume-based: microliters per liter), mg/kg (mass-based: milligrams per kilogram), and µmol/mol (mole-based: micromole per mole) among others.

Effects of Carbon Dioxide on the Brain at Different Concentrations

200ppm-300ppm

This was the average carbon dioxide concentration out in nature before the industrial revolution. At this level, there are no immediate effects on cognition.

Around 400ppm

This is the present-day average carbon dioxide concentration out in nature. In reality, it is a little bit higher than that (417ppm as of December 2021).

500ppm-900ppm

In large cities, carbon dioxide concentrations can reach levels as high as these. At this point, there aren't any immediately harmful effects on a person's wellbeing; however, it has been shown that a person's decision-making skills are somewhat affected at these concentrations (Heid, 2012).

1000ppm-1400ppm (0.1% - 0.14%)

However, the story does not end there. In smaller, poorly ventilated spaces, such as schools, the carbon dioxide concentration can reach 1000ppm. According to Scott & Baute (2019), we can observe a 15% decrease in cognitive function at this level.



At 1400ppm, we can observe a 50% decrease in cognitive function.

2000ppm (0.2%)

Here, some side effects start to appear. These include nausea, loss of attention, and poor concentration, among others. The scary thing is that carbon dioxide levels of 2000ppm are relatively common. The concentration of carbon dioxide can reach 4000ppm in cars and bedrooms (Scott & Baute, 2019).

5000ppm (0.5%)

This is the maximum limit imposed by the Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) for the average carbon dioxide concentration over a period of 8 hours. It is generally not advisable to do work at levels as high as these.



Interestingly, in the video I embedded at the start of this article, Kurtis Baute (the speaker) exposed himself to a carbon dioxide concentration of around 6500ppm. During the video, he can be seen struggling to remember parts of the script (2:29). He also stated that he had to do multiple takes for the video because of the high carbon dioxide concentration (3:15).

10000ppm (1%)

Here, drowsiness is possible. Surprisingly, levels as high as these still occur in our everyday lives. For instance, motorcycle helmets can contain air with a carbon dioxide concentration of 10000ppm (1%). Now consider that these people not only have to breathe in air with a lot of carbon dioxide but also drive around a town or city. It is not an ideal situation to be in.

30000ppm (3%)

This is the maximum limit recommended by the American Conference of Governmental Industrial Hygienists (ACGIH) for the average carbon dioxide concentration over a period of 10 minutes.



At this level, there are also effects on other parts of the body, such as the respiratory and circulatory systems. Increased heart rate and blood pressure have been reported at these levels (ESHG, n.d.).

40000ppm (4%)

Levels these high are immediately dangerous and may result in death.

50000ppm (5%)

According to the FSIS Environmental Safety and Health Group [ESHG] (n.d.), exposure to these levels of concentration leads to "strong respiratory stimulation, dizziness, confusion, headache, [and] shortness of breath".

80000ppm (8%)

According to the FSIS Environmental Safety and Health Group [ESHG] (n.d.), exposure to these levels of concentration leads to "dimmed sight, sweating, tremor, unconsciousness, and possible death".

What this Means for Us

With carbon dioxide concentrations set to reach values as high as 695ppm by the end of this century, our entire species may experience a 10-16% decrease in our cognitive ability (Scott & Baute, 2019).



This is rather concerning, however, there are things that we can do to lessen the detrimental effects of carbon dioxide in our lives. Here are some of the things that we can do:

  • Open a window
  • Get some plants
  • Check your ventilation systems
  • Use fans to ventilate air around appliances


In conclusion, due to climate change, carbon dioxide concentrations are increasing worldwide. This is worrying because high carbon dioxide concentrations have adverse effects on our bodies; however, there are many ways to counteract these negative effects, such as opening windows, getting some plants, etc.



So open a window and let the fresh air in! Breathe in. Breathe out.
